In 1917, an artist presented a sclpture of a urinal. It was recently sold for 2 million dollars. How did he do it?

  • The artist Marcel Duchamp presented an sculpture named Fountain, a porcelain urinal signed "R.Mutt. Duchamp questioned “Can one make works which are not ‘of art’”
  • After photographing the piece, it was immediately disposed
  • At the beginning he was dubbed as an “amateur inventor”
  • But he transformed the meaning of his sculpture Fountain with his own method of distribution
  • He distributed photographs of the work through various media
  • Its bluntness intrigued the public. They asked why was this mundane object all over the place?
  • This raised intrigue
  • Distribution
  • It was the thing that played the most with the Fountain;s acquisition of value
  • Does one have an obligation to view the work first-hand?
  • What happens when a more intimate, thoughtful, and enduring understanding comes from mediated discussions of an exhibition, rather than from a direct experience of the work?
  • Is it incumbent upon the consumer to bear witness, or can one’s art experience derive from magazines, the Internet, books, and conversation?
  • We should recognize that collective experience is now based on simultaneous private experiences, distributed across the field of media culture, knit together by ongoing debate, publicity, promotion and discussion
  • The secret?
    • Distribute an intriguing object as much as you can and its value will rise over time
